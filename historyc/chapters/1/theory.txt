    <h2 id="history">History and Evolution</h2>
    <p>
        The <strong>Customer Information Control System (CICS)</strong> was developed by IBM in <strong>1968</strong> 
        as an online transaction processing (OLTP) monitor for the <strong>System/360</strong> mainframe. Originally 
        designed to handle simple batch-to-online conversions for banking and insurance, it quickly became the 
        backbone of enterprise computing.<sup>[1][2]</sup>
    </p>

    <p>
        The first release of CICS provided support for terminal control, task management, and basic file access. 
        As industries demanded higher reliability and scalability, IBM introduced major enhancements including 
        program isolation, recovery mechanisms, and database integration during the 1970s.<sup>[3]</sup> 
        CICS became synonymous with reliability in high-volume transaction environments.
    </p>

        <img src="images/cics.png" alt="CICS on System/360" width="500">

    <p>
        Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, CICS evolved into a full-featured transaction manager, introducing 
        structured programming interfaces, MRO (Multi-Region Operation), and support for distributed processing. 
        The launch of <strong>CICS Transaction Server (TS)</strong> in 1998 marked its transformation into a 
        multi-language, internet-enabled platform.<sup>[4][5]</sup>
    </p>

    <h2 id="features">Architecture and Core Capabilities</h2>
    <p>
        CICS operates as a transaction monitor managing thousands of concurrent users and tasks. It follows a 
        modular, multi-region architecture supporting transaction routing, intercommunication, and dynamic program 
        loading. Its design ensures atomicity, consistency, isolation, and durability (ACID) for mission-critical 
        operations.<sup>[6]</sup>
    </p>

    <p>
        It supports multiple programming languages including <code>COBOL</code>, <code>PL/I</code>, <code>C</code>, 
        <code>Java</code>, and <code>Assembler</code>. CICS’s core services—such as <code>EXEC CICS</code> APIs—allow 
        programs to perform file handling, communication, and data management with high efficiency.<sup>[7]</sup>
    </p>

    <p>
        Over time, CICS integrated modern technologies including <strong>Web services (SOAP/REST)</strong>, 
        <strong>JVM Server</strong> support, and <strong>MQ messaging</strong>. These advancements positioned CICS as 
        a hybrid transaction platform bridging traditional mainframe workloads and modern cloud-native applications.<sup>[8][9]</sup>
    </p>

    <h2 id="integration">Integration with IBM Mainframe</h2>
    <p>
        CICS remains deeply integrated with IBM mainframe environments such as <code>z/OS</code> and 
        <code>z/VSE</code>. It interacts seamlessly with <code>DB2</code>, <code>IMS</code>, and 
        <code>MQ</code> to manage complex business transactions securely and efficiently.<sup>[5][7]</sup>
    </p>


    <p>
        Using <strong>CICS Transaction Server</strong> and <strong>CICS Explorer</strong>, developers can design, 
        deploy, and monitor applications with high availability and performance. The platform’s integration with 
        <strong>IBM Language Environment (LE)</strong> enables mixed-language application development, ensuring 
        long-term compatibility across enterprise systems.<sup>[8][10]</sup>
    </p>

    <h2 id="legacy">Legacy and Modern Relevance</h2>
    <p>
        Today, CICS powers billions of transactions daily in industries such as banking, retail, and logistics. 
        It underpins critical systems like ATM networks, airline reservation systems, and insurance platforms.<sup>[2][9]</sup>
    </p>

    <p>
        IBM continues to modernize CICS with cloud, API, and DevOps integrations through <strong>CICS TS for z/OS 6.1</strong>. 
        The system’s resilience, performance, and backward compatibility ensure that CICS remains a foundational 
        component of mainframe-based digital transformation.<sup>[10]</sup>
    </p>
